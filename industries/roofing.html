Storm demand does not trickle. It floods.

Roofing is the only trade where a single night of weather can produce a month of phone calls, and most of them come in a window measured in days.

Everyone's phone rings at once, and briefly

After hail moves through, homeowners work down a list. They call, and if nobody picks up, they call the next roofer on that list. The company that answers first usually gets the inspection, and the inspection is where the job is won. Two weeks later the wave is over and the phones go quiet again.

Your best people are on roofs, not at desks

The exact moment the calls surge is the exact moment your crews and estimators are up on decking with nail guns running. Nobody should be reaching for a phone on a ridge line, and nobody can hear one over a compressor. So the calls ring out, and ringing out is the same as saying no.

Leak calls do not wait for a callback

A homeowner watching water come through a ceiling isn't leaving a voicemail and hoping. They are calling roofers until a voice says "we can help." If that voice is not yours, the job, the referral, and the insurance work that follows all go to whoever answered.

You cannot staff for a spike

Hiring a full-time receptionist to cover a two-week storm surge means paying a salary through every quiet month in between. Most roofing companies split the difference: the office manager drowns during the surge, and calls still get missed. The math has never worked, which is why it keeps not getting solved.

Roofer questions

Can it keep up with call volume after a hail storm?

Yes. It is software, not one person at a desk, so a post-storm surge does not turn into a hold queue or a busy signal. It answers every call, 24/7, including the ones that come in at 9 pm while you are still tarping roofs. Every booking lands on your calendar and every call gets a transcript you can read that night.

What happens when a caller has an active leak?

Calls about water actively coming into a house are treated as emergencies. The receptionist transfers real emergencies to a human on your team, and you decide which number it rings. Everything else gets booked or taken as a message so your phone only interrupts you when it should.

Can it answer insurance and adjuster questions?

No, and it does not pretend to. Questions about claims, deductibles, supplements, or adjuster meetings are taken down as detailed messages for a human on your team to return. The caller hears that a person will call them back, and you get the details in the transcript instead of a vague voicemail.
